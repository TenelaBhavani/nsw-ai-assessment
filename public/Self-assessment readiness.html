The start of the self-assessment

Framework benefits and risks overview

Benefits and risks

AI can amplify existing risks; this means you need to carefully consider the risks and benefits.

Currently, we use AI tools to:

  • Deliver insights that improve services and lives
  • Help agencies work more quickly and accurately

While there are many areas where AI can benefit the work we do, we need to engage with risks early and throughout the solution lifecycle.

⚠ Cannot answer some questions?

It is important to make a note of questions you cannot answer as you progress through the assessment.

If the project/system proceeds, treat these unanswered questions as representing mid-range risk, commence with a pilot phase and closely monitor for harms and establish controls.

Understanding the AI self-assessment process

This AI Assessment Framework is structured in sections that align to the AI Ethics Principles.

Each section contains questions for assessing specific risks and advise actions for mitigation.

At the conclusion of the self-assessment, you will confirm the highest residual risk rating for each of the five Ethics Principles.

ℹ The balance of benefits and risks

Some elevated risk uses of AI are undertaken to improve existing processes, or because of a clear benefit to community. It is important to consider the risk of not using AI if it is the best solution available.

Ensuring commitment to Human Rights

Identify any potential risk to human rights

  • Is the AI system likely to restrict human rights? If so, is it publicly justifiable?
  • Were possible trade-offs between principles and rights documented and evaluated?
  • Does the AI system suggest decisions or outline choices to users?
  • Could the AI system impact users’ autonomy or influence decision-making?
  • Are users informed that outputs arise from algorithmic decisions?

Do I need a Human Rights Impact Assessment (HRIA)?

If the assessment indicates potential human rights risks, conduct a Human Rights Impact Assessment (HRIA) before proceeding.

Ensure AI use complies with legal protections

Human rights are legally recognised and protected through:

  • Laws at federal and state levels
  • The Australian Constitution
  • The common law

Applicable federal and state laws include:

  • Australian Human Rights Commission Act 1986 (Cth)
  • Age Discrimination Act 2004 (Cth)
  • Disability Discrimination Act 1992 (Cth)
  • Racial Discrimination Act 1975 (Cth)
  • Sex Discrimination Act 1984 (Cth)
  • Anti-Discrimination Act 1977 (NSW)

Publicly available resources:

  • Australian Human Rights Commission
  • Public Sector Guidance Sheets

Identifying responsible officers

Reviewing potential AI risks requires individuals who are appropriately skilled and qualified. In this framework, these roles are referred to as responsible officers.

The self-assessment must be completed by (or confirmed with) the Responsible Officers. These roles cover project leadership, technical performance and data governance.

These four roles have independent responsibilities and must not all be held by the same person*.

Understanding the risk levels

ℹ️

Determine your risk levels

Given the absence of a standard for AI risk levels, use this foundation to build on. The self-assessment employs these concepts.

Consider elevated risk factors, reversibility, and your business use case when establishing risk levels.

The examples provided may shift between risk levels.


The None, negligible,
or N/A Risk

Definition: AI systems or applications that have no, or extremely minimal risk associated with their use, or where the concept of risk is not applicable due to the nature of the AI system or its intended purpose.



Consequences: The potential consequences of an AI system in this category are either non-existent or so insignificant that they can be safely disregarded.

Low Risk:
Reversible with negligible consequences

Definition: AI systems or applications that, if they malfunction or produce unintended outcomes, can be easily reversed or corrected without causing any harm or damage.





Consequences: The potential consequences of a low-risk AI system are minimal and do not cause any harm on individuals, organisations, or society. 

Mid-range Risk:
Reversible with moderate consequences

Definition: AI systems or applications that, if they malfunction or produce unintended outcomes, can be reversed or corrected, but may cause moderate inconvenience, disruption, or harm.



Consequences: The potential consequences of a mid-range risk AI system are more noticeable and may have a temporary impact on individuals, organisations, or specific domains. 

High Risk:
Reversible with significant consequences

Definition: AI systems or applications that, if they malfunction or produce unintended outcomes, can be reversed or corrected, but may cause significant financial losses, reputational damage, harm to the environment, individuals, or society.

Consequences: The potential consequences of a high-risk AI system are substantial and may have a lasting impact on individuals, organisations, or entire industries. 

Very High Risk:
Significant or irreversible consequences

Definition:AI systems or applications that, if they malfunction or produce unintended outcomes, may cause catastrophic, irreversible consequences for individuals, societies, or the environment



Consequences: The potential consequences of a very high-risk AI system are severe and may have permanent and irreversible implications.

Understanding the risk levels (continued) - Examples

ℹ️

NOTE

Please use the following as a general guide only, examples can move between risk levels as they would be dependent on specific use cases


The None, negligible,
or N/A Risk

Can’t create harm





Examples:
- Noise suppression on audio calls
- Image resolution enhancements
- Grammer and spell checking
- Text summarisation of non-sensitive content
- Search functions in browsers
- Analytics report

Low Risk:
Reversible with negligible consequences

Can be reversed or corrected without causing harm.



Examples:
- Anomaly detection software.
- Email spam filters
- Document classification and tagging
- Photo organising
- Non-critical content translation
- Voice assistance for basic tasks, i.e. Automated phone menu

Mid-range Risk:
Reversible with moderate consequences

Can be reversed or corrected but may cause inconvenience, disruption or harm.


Examples:
- Customer service chatbots
- Recommendation systems
- Language translation tools
- Content curation
- Predictive maintenance
- Natural language processing of gov documents.

High Risk:
Reversible with significant consequences

Can be reversed but may cause significant damage or harm with potential lasting impact.

Examples:
- Facial recognition systems.
- AI powered hiring and recruitment
- Autonomous emergency response system
- Autonomous tram with human oversight
- Healthcare decision support systems.
- Adaptive learning system

Very High Risk:
Significant or irreversible consequences

Cannot be reversed, may cause permanent or irreversible harms.



Examples:
- Autonomous benefits eligibility without human oversight
- Self-driving cars
- Predictive reoffending
- Medical diagnosis without oversight
- Autonomous AI systems on critical infrastructure (i.e. energy)
